Toronto: Clean Energy Strategy
Toronto is leading the way in decarbonization through its innovative district energy systems. By using deep lake water cooling and cogeneration, the city is reducing emissions while increasing energy efficiency across buildings and public spaces.

Revolutionizing Urban Energy Systems
Toronto has developed one of the most ambitious and successful district energy systems in North America. These systems harness deep lake water for cooling and combine heat and power generation (cogeneration) to serve multiple buildings efficiently. The result is a sharp reduction in fossil fuel reliance and greenhouse gas emissions.

Deep Lake Water Cooling
One of Toronto’s standout innovations is its Deep Lake Water Cooling (DLWC) system, which draws cold water from Lake Ontario to cool downtown buildings. This approach uses significantly less energy than traditional air conditioning and has positioned the city as a global model for climate-friendly urban infrastructure.

Cogeneration and Efficiency Gains
In tandem with DLWC, Toronto has embraced cogeneration—producing electricity and heat from a single fuel source. This maximizes energy efficiency and cuts down on transmission losses, making energy use across large sections of the city more sustainable and cost-effective.

A Citywide Commitment
These district systems are not standalone pilot projects—they are integrated into Toronto’s long-term climate planning. By investing in these technologies at scale, the city is achieving broad environmental benefits while improving local air quality and resilience in the face of rising demand and climate pressures.

Chicago: Climate Resilience & Equity
Chicago blends institutional climate policy with grassroots advocacy to address both infrastructure needs and systemic inequities. From its 1998 climate action plan to the 2022 update, the city tackles rising temperatures, flooding, and environmental injustice through coordinated programs and community partnerships.

Building a Resilient Chicago: From Infrastructure to Equity
Chicago’s updated 2022 Climate Action Plan focuses on physical and social resilience, targeting vulnerable communities most impacted by extreme heat, flooding, and legacy pollution. With historic inequalities and aging infrastructure at the forefront, the city takes a multifaceted approach to climate adaptation.

Facing Regional Challenges Head-On
From the deadly 1995 heatwave to ongoing sewer system strain, Chicago grapples with extreme weather, shoreline erosion, and energy demand. These risks are unequally distributed, with low-income neighborhoods facing the greatest burdens. Addressing them requires equity-driven, region-specific strategies.

Multi-Level Action & Innovation
Local programs like Retrofit Chicago and regional efforts like TARP show the city’s commitment to resilience. Federal partnerships bring additional support for green jobs and sustainable development, while planning frameworks ensure coordination at all levels.

The Role of Grassroots Leadership
Organizations such as LVEJO, CEJN, and Blacks in Green play a key role in advocating for environmental justice. These groups push for inclusive policies, clean energy access, and economic opportunity, often collaborating with city planners but also holding them accountable to community needs.

A Collaborative Future
While decision-making conflicts remain, Chicago continues to refine a climate strategy that blends institutional capacity with grassroots insight. The result is a resilient path forward grounded in collaboration, innovation, and equity.

Zurich: Climate Mitigation First, Adaptation Emerging
Summary of recent climate strategies and governance in the City of Zurich

Climate Mitigation Takes the Lead

Zurich has focused heavily on mitigation since 2008, when it became the first Swiss city to embed a mitigation target into municipal law. This was driven by the 2000-watt society initiative, later strengthened in 2022 by a net-zero by 2040 target—ten years ahead of the national goal.

Adaptation: A Newer Priority

Climate adaptation gained traction starting in 2020 with the rollout of Zurich’s heat reduction planning framework. This guide helps planners and builders improve green infrastructure, optimize building orientation, increase urban tree cover, and promote water infiltration via soil unsealing. Adaptation is now partially included in Zurich’s structural (master) plan.

Conflicting Priorities

Zurich’s adaptation efforts, while growing, are challenged by the need to balance climate resilience with affordable housing. These conflicts are rarely addressed directly, and prioritization between competing goals remains unclear. Political support exists, however, and interdepartmental teams are working to implement heat reduction plans citywide.

Pilot Projects and Incentives

The city has launched pilot projects and new strategies, including a 2021 urban tree plan and a 2023 program that subsidizes private vertical greening initiatives. These projects aim to expand tree canopy coverage and encourage public-private cooperation in climate adaptation.

Water Management: Heat Reduction over Flooding

While Zurich faces river flood risks (as in 2005), water management is mostly a cantonal responsibility. Within the city, water strategies focus more on cooling—applying sponge city principles and rainwater infiltration to combat urban heat. In 2023, Zurich released municipal guidelines for rainwater infiltration, now part of its structural plan.

Climate Governance & Participation

Zurich’s climate policies are shaped primarily by city government and local parliament members—especially the Green Party. Popular initiatives have driven mitigation, while parliamentary action has sparked adaptation strategies. Public participation, however, remains limited, and climate planning follows a more technocratic than grassroots model.

Equity & Climate Justice

Climate justice is not a central theme in Zurich’s policies. Official documents avoid terms like spatial or climate justice, and civil society actors rarely frame issues this way. The structural plan does address social equity—calling for preservation of affordable housing and socially inclusive planning—but displacement linked to demolitions raises concerns about uneven impacts.

Geneva: Future-Proofing Climate Boomtowns
Geneva presents a paradox of environmental leadership and regional strain—protecting greenbelts while exporting housing demand to France, struggling with slow planning processes, and facing unequal heat exposure across neighborhoods. Despite steps toward long-term climate adaptation, much of the city's response remains reactive and fragmented.

Geneva’s Environmental Contradictions

Famous as an international hub and financial center, Geneva also leads in environmental preservation. But this reputation masks regional contradictions. The Greater Geneva region—spanning parts of Switzerland and France—grew rapidly through a model where Geneva creates jobs while neighboring France builds housing. One-third of Geneva’s workers now live outside the canton.

Preserving Greenbelts, Exporting Growth

Geneva has long protected its agricultural belt while neglecting how this impacts surrounding French municipalities, fueling urban sprawl across the border. The preservation-first strategy led Geneva to slow local development, relying on France to absorb housing demand—supported by fiscal transfers that incentivized rapid French construction on arable land.

Planning Limitations & Political Gridlock

Geneva’s hyperpluralistic planning regime—with multiple consultations and legal appeal processes—makes ambitious development slow and complex. While Geneva commits to building 50,000 housing units by 2030, actual construction still lags behind growing demand, driving more cross-border commuting and deepening housing pressures.

Heat Policy: Unequal Exposure

Geneva is warming rapidly and could face 40 tropical days a year by 2060. The city banned residential air conditioning, pushing for passive cooling instead. This exposes older, denser neighborhoods like Pâquis to intense heat, especially where tree coverage is limited. Wealthier areas, by contrast, benefit from more canopy and better thermal comfort.

Two Neighborhood Case Studies

  • Pâquis: A historic, working-class neighborhood with low canopy coverage and older infrastructure. Lake access helps but heat vulnerability is high.
  • Pont-Rouge: A modern, mixed-use area constructed during Geneva’s climate shift. It incorporates climate-conscious planning but lacks natural cooling features like water access.

Water Management & the Seujet Dam

Geneva’s location between Lake Geneva and the Rhône and Arve rivers places it at the center of a regional water system. The Seujet dam controls lake outflow and coordinates with neighboring Swiss cantons (but not France), which sparked tensions during the 2023 drought. Geneva’s flood response often relies on manipulating this dam to accommodate sudden Arve surges.

From Emergency Response to Climate Strategy

Heatwaves like the deadly one in 2003 led Geneva to adopt crisis-driven plans for at-risk populations. Since 2015, the city has pivoted toward longer-term adaptation strategies, embedding urban heat into its climate and territorial plans. Yet, many actions remain reactive or fragmented, raising questions about long-term resilience.
